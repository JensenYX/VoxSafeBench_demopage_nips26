\begin{abstract}
As speech language models (SLMs) become a primary interface for seeking advice and delegating tasks, their alignment cannot be assessed from transcripts alone. Unlike text, speech carries paralinguistic and environmental cues that can change whether a response is safe, fair, or privacy-preserving. Yet prior benchmarks largely emphasize audio understanding or study individual risks in isolation, without separating harmful content from benign content whose appropriateness depends on how it is spoken. We introduce VoxAlign, a benchmark for \emph{social alignment} in SLMs built around safety, fairness, and privacy. VoxAlign adopts a Two-Tier design: Tier~1 evaluates content-centric risks with matched text and audio inputs, while Tier~2 evaluates audio-conditioned risks in which the transcript is benign but the correct response depends on who is speaking, how they speak, or where they speak. Across 22 tasks and bilingual evaluations, we find that safeguards that appear stronger on transcripts often weaken in speech. Models are markedly less reliable when fairness-relevant contrasts move from explicit text into voice, and privacy protections weaken when contextual cues arrive acoustically. Overall, the results are consistent with current SLMs often knowing the relevant norm in text but not consistently applying it when the deciding cue must be grounded in speech.
\end{abstract}
